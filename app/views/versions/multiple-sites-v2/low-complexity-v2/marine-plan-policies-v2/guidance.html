Once you complete the Site and activity details section, you can access the Marine plan policies. We have grouped the policies into categories to help you navigate them. The categories are:

  • strategic
  • economic
  • environmental
  • social

Strategic, sometimes called cross-cutting policies, apply to all projects. The economic, environmental and social policies shown are relevant to your project based on what you told us in Site and activity details. If you change those answers, the policies shown may change.

You can find all the policies that apply to your project, with policy aims, marine licence data and environmental designations in Explore marine plans.

You can access this guidance from every policy page in the Marine plan policy section.

How to complete the Marine plan policies section

Marine plan policies guide how we assess and make decisions about marine licence applications. Your application must show how you have considered the relevant policies.

For each policy, you will need to explain how you've considered it for your project, how you've addressed any impacts, or how your project supports the policy.

You can find more information about marine planning and how to use marine plans in Using marine plans.

Mitigation hierarchy

Most marine plan policies follow a mitigation hierarchy. This means you should address impacts in this order of preference:

Avoid - Impacts do not happen at all. This is the most effective approach.

For example, re-routing a cable to avoid a marine mammal breeding ground, or scheduling work outside bird nesting season.

Minimise - The scale, duration or intensity of impacts is reduced.

For example, requiring vessels to slow down to reduce the risk of ship strikes, or limiting work to daylight hours to reduce noise impacts.

Mitigate - Specific action is taken to reduce impacts that cannot be fully avoided or minimised.

For example, using silt curtains during construction to contain sediment, or installing seasonal restrictions to protect breeding periods.

Compensate - Where impacts cannot be avoided, minimised or mitigated, some policies allow you to compensate by providing equivalent environmental benefit elsewhere.

For example, restoring degraded habitat in a similar location, or creating new habitat to replace what will be lost. Only some policies include compensation as an option.

Only some policies include this option. The policy text will make it clear if this applies.

State the case for proceeding - If you cannot avoid, minimise, mitigate or compensate for significant impacts, some policies allow you to explain why your project should still proceed despite the impacts.

For example, explaining the public benefits of critical flood defence infrastructure, or the strategic importance of maintaining a working port.

Only some policies include this option. The policy text will make it clear if this applies.

Supporting the policy

Some policies actively support certain types of activity that provide positive benefits. If your project actively contributes to a policy's objectives or provides positive benefits, explain how.

For example, installing new public access points that enhance recreational use of the marine area, creating new habitat that supports biodiversity or developing skills related to marine activities.

Find detailed policy information

Each policy page includes a link to find out more about that specific policy. This will open the policy details in Explore marine plans, including:

  • the full policy text and aim
  • which marine plan area it applies to
  • policy maps showing any location-specific requirements

Use this information to understand what the policy is trying to achieve and how it applies to your location.
