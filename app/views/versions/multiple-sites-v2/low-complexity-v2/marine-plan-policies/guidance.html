How to complete the Marine plan policies section

For most marine plan policies, you'll need to explain how the project will manage its impacts by completing text boxes for:

  • avoiding impacts
  • minimising impacts
  • mitigating impacts
  • compensating for impacts (or stating the case for proceeding)

Where possible, avoid impacts. If you cannot avoid impacts, minimise them. If impacts remain, mitigate them. Only compensate as a last resort. If you will not be compensating for the impacts you can state the case for proceeding

Avoiding impacts

This means that the impacts do not happen. You should prioritise avoiding impacts when possible as it's the most effective step you can take.

For example, re-routing a sub-sea cable to avoid a known marine mammal breeding ground.

Minimising impacts

This means that the scale, duration or intensity of the impacts is reduced.

For example, requiring vessels to slow down to minimise the risk of ship strikes on whales or dolphins.

Mitigating impacts

This means that specific action is taken to reduce impacts that cannot be fully avoided or minimised.

For example, using seasonal restrictions to avoid breeding periods, or installing silt curtains during construction to contain sediment disturbance.

Some policies require specific mitigations - these are shown alongside policies this applies to.

Compensating for impacts (or stating the case for proceeding)

This means providing an equivalent environmental benefit elsewhere to offset unavoidable impacts. This should only be used as a last resort.

For example, restoring degraded habitat in a similar location, or creating new habitat to replace what will be lost.

If you will not be compensating for the impacts, use this section to state the case for proceeding.

Some policies do not include compensation as an option. If this applies, you'll only see avoid, minimise and mitigate.

Some policies do not follow this structure and only require an explanation of your considerations.
